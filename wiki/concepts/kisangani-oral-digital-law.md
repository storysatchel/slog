# Kisangani Oral-Digital Legal Framework

## Core Concept
In the Kisangani fleet, law is not a static, top-down paper edict, but an active, relational state of mutual assent (*chibvumano*). Grounded in the segmentary lineage-based governance of the historical Mutapa Empire, this consensus operates through a highly advanced **oral-digital hybrid tradition**.

## The Guardians of the Law: Masvikiro and N'anga
The *masvikiro* (spirit mediums) and *n'anga* (healers/diviners) act as the conscious guardians and the "living breath" of the law. They are highly respected human agents, completely eschewing dehumanizing cybernetic implants (like neural interfaces) in favor of mastering advanced external tools. 

When establishing or reciting the lineage of agreements, they wield specialized external technology—such as ceremonial hand terminals shaped to echo the soapstone birds of Great Zimbabwe, and AR headgear etched with the chevron masonry patterns of the *Imba Huru*. These devices use biometric and vocal signatures to securely authenticate their words, seamlessly projecting their spoken consensus into the fleet's decentralized *chizivo* (database). They are never subsumed by the technology; they wield it to mediate between the living, the *mhondoro* (ancestors), and the divine.

## The Sacred Period of Settling (Light-Speed Delay)
Adhering to the hard sci-fi physics of *The Expanse* universe, the *chizivo* operates over a decentralized network constrained by light-speed delay. Broadcasting a legal consensus across the vast distances separating the Kisangani fleet can take minutes, hours, or even days.

This physical limitation is not viewed as a weakness. Instead, it has been embraced and culturally codified. Just as communication and tribute in the Mutapa Empire required overland caravans to traverse the Zambezi valley, the transmission delay in space is ritually observed as the **"sacred period of settling."** It is a deliberate, culturally grounded period that allows communal consensus to organically take root across the fleet's segmentary kinship network before a *kibvumano* is fully recognized.

## Handling Dissent
The *chizivo* database does not track law as a binary "valid" or "invalid" state. Instead, it acts as a real-time diagnostic heatmap of relational harmony. When a faction or ship withdraws their assent, it registers as a "fracture" or "dissonance" in the interface. 

This weakening of consensus does not trigger a punitive, carceral response. Rather, it serves as an alarm indicating that the community's relationship is ailing, which triggers a **dare** (traditional council assembly). The *masvikiro* and elders convene to mediate and heal the rift, ensuring the law remains a living agreement rather than a coercive tool.

## Narrative Tension and Resiliency
It is critical to avoid framing this consensus-based governance as inherently fragile or perpetually on the brink of catastrophic collapse, which risks echoing colonial tropes about non-Western political structures. Confederal, kinship-based systems like those of the historical Mutapa state were incredibly resilient, designed to accommodate prolonged negotiation, overlapping authorities, and sustained debate without shattering.

The narrative tension of the Kisangani legal system does not stem from the immediate threat of the fleet being "torn apart." Instead, the tension arises from the profound, exhaustive, and rigorous communal labor the *dare* must undertake. Mediating a fractured *kibvumano* requires navigating complex lineages, honoring overlapping jurisdictions (such as those of the various *madzishe* or provincial chiefs), and investing significant spiritual and emotional energy to restore the living breath of the law.
